RISC-V’s adoption is paced by the availability of supporting CPUs, SOCs and SOMs, which are, in turn, constrained by the peripherals required for the target application. Proprietary peripheral timelines are currently dictated by driver development, which in Linux, is directly tied to the supporting kernel versions. Consequently, writing custom drivers effectively forks the kernel at a selected version and immediately incurs the vendor’s perpetual obligation to support this orphaned kernel for the entire product life cycle, including the necessity to backport any subsequent version releases for security reasons or otherwise. The weight of this obligation multiplies with the proliferation of FPGAs and their tightly-coupled development tools. The result is a massive, monolithic build that is perpetually frozen in time with no possibility of incremental version control, let alone multi-developer collaboration on functionally-independent modules. Linux’s open source promise has become the RISC-V industry’s largest liability.

Minix-V relieves all of these constraints through a simple duality of enduring design principles: separation of concerns and information hiding. Both principles manifest directly in the microkernel architecture. The following table enumerates the particulars by which this long-obvious architectural paradigm translates into massive product, market and financial advantages. The first vendor to adopt this change will secure a leadership position among all RISC-V designers, manufacturers, and customers.

The balance of this document outlines a strategy for enabling and expediting the Minix-V adoption, followed by a proposal for a selected vendor to define a new level of industry leadership through orders-of-magnitude productivity, functionality and quality gains.
